HEAT INDEX

Extreme heat or a high heat index refers to a period of excessively hot weather, often accompanied by high humidity, which makes it feel hotter than the actual air temperature. The heat index is a measure that combines air temperature to determine how hot it actually feels to the human body.
In Pasay City, located in Metro Manila, the heat index has become a serious concern, especially during the dry season (March to May). According to PAGASA (Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration), Pasay has experienced heat index levels exceeding 42°C, which falls under the "danger" category. At this level, heat cramps and heat exhaustion are likely, and heat stroke becomes possible with prolonged exposure or physical activity.

In April and May 2024, PAGASA recorded heat indices in Pasay between 40°C and 46°C. These levels led to the suspension of afternoon classes, increased cases of heat-related illnesses, and higher electricity usage due to reliance on air conditioning and fans. Extreme heat is not just a discomfort, it poses serious health risks, especially to the elderly, children, outdoor workers, and people with chronic illnesses.

What happens when it gets too hot?

If the heat index goes over 42°C, PAGASA issues a "danger" warning. Once it hits 52°C, it enters the "extreme danger" level, where heat stroke is likely—even with short exposure. During these times, the government may suspend classes, cancel outdoor work, or open cooling shelters and evacuation centers..

Heat Index Risk Levels

  • Caution (27°C–32°C): Increased sweating, fatigue
  • Extreme Caution (33°C–41°C): Possible cramps, exhaustion
  • Danger (42°C–51°C): Heat stroke becomes likely
  • Extreme Danger (52°C and up): Heat stroke highly likely

When danger levels are reached, agencies give public alerts and prepare evacuation centers. These centers usually have electric fans or air-conditioning, cold drinking water, first-aid kits, and trained health workers. Some LGUs use open gyms or barangay halls so more people can stay cool safely. For example, Iloilo City allocated PHP 43 million to build heat-safe evacuation centers in different barangays due to worsening summer heat

Agencies

In the Philippines, the weather can get dangerously hot, especially during dry seasons or when climate events like El Nino happen. When both temperature and humidity are high, people don't just feel uncomfortable, it can actually be life threatening. The heat index is what tells us how hot it feels outside. For example, if it says 36°C on the weather app, it could actually feel like 45°C if the air is really humid.

When it's very humid, our sweat doesn't evaporate properly. And if we can't cool down, our body temperature rises. That's when people start to feel dizzy, tired, and sick. Some even faint or end up in the hospital. That's why evacuation centers are sometimes opened not just for floods or typhoons, but for heat too. These centers offer air-conditioned or ventilated spaces, drinking water, and medical help when needed. Government agencies rely on heat index data to make these decisions and keep people safe.

What are the things to do before, during and after the disaster strikes?



before

  • - Remove excess and tight clothing for airflow
  • Stay hydrated and drink a lot of water regularly
  • avoid doing outdoor activities when it is peak heat hours (10 am up to 4 pm)

During

  • Apply cold compress to wrists/neck
  • Move to cooler place
  • Use fans, cool sprays
  • Elevate legs if someone faints

After

  • Keep hydrated
  • Monitor symptoms
  • Seek medical help if needed
